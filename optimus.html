Business Case

Last-mile delivery is
broken in Bangladesh.

Bangladesh's e-commerce and F-commerce sector has grown explosively — but the logistics infrastructure supporting it has barely kept pace. Merchants selling on Facebook, Daraz, or their own storefronts depend entirely on third-party courier networks that are opaque, slow, and expensive. For banks and corporate clients dispatching field documentation, the pain is even sharper: no tracking, no proof of delivery, and no recourse when things go wrong.

The core tension is this: delivery companies in Bangladesh operate with manual order sorting, gut-feel routing, and no real-time visibility — meaning riders waste fuel criss-crossing the same neighbourhoods, customers wait with no ETA, and merchants absorb the cost of failed deliveries through returned COD orders that never convert.

For F-commerce merchants — the fastest-growing seller segment — cash-on-delivery is the dominant payment model, making every failed or delayed delivery a direct revenue loss. For banks and corporate clients, delayed document delivery compounds operational SLA breaches and customer dissatisfaction at scale. Optimus was built to fix both.

Most delivery businesses treat routing as an afterthought — riders plan their own routes intuitively. The result is duplicated coverage, wasted kilometres, and delivery windows that slip by hours. The opportunity was to bring the same geographic intelligence Barikoi applied to CPV verification into the last-mile logistics stack.

— Optimus Product Discovery, 2024
01
Manual, Inefficient Order Sorting
Dispatchers manually assigned orders to riders based on memory and habit — no geographic logic, no load balancing, no priority handling. Peak-hour backlogs were routine.
02
Zero Route Intelligence
Riders planned their own routes, often backtracking across the same areas. No traffic data, no multi-stop optimization, no eco-routing — just guesswork burning fuel and time.
03
No Customer Visibility
Customers had no live tracking, no ETA, and no way to confirm delivery. Trust in online merchants depended on delivery experience — and it was consistently poor.
04
Fraud & Disputed Deliveries
Without proof-of-delivery — no photo, no signature, no GPS timestamp — merchants and banks had no recourse against fraudulent non-delivery claims from either riders or customers.
05
Siloed Financial Visibility
Revenue, COD collections, expenses, and rider performance were tracked in spreadsheets. Finance teams had no real-time view of delivery economics or profitability by zone.
User Research

Three types of users,
one broken experience.

Delivery management systems serve a triangle of stakeholders — each with distinct goals that are often in tension. Getting the product right meant understanding all three simultaneously and designing features that served each without compromising the others.

M
The Merchant
F-commerce Seller / Business Owner
Needs COD collection to be fast and reconciled daily — every delayed return is frozen working capital.
Wants visibility into which orders are delivered, failed, or in transit — without calling the courier company.
Needs delivery charges to be transparent and predictable — surprise costs erode margins on thin F-commerce economics.
Wants proof of delivery so disputed orders don't default in the customer's favour.
R
The Rider
Delivery Agent / Field Staff
Needs a clear, ordered trip list — too many manual decisions per shift leads to missed deliveries and complaints.
Wants an optimized route so they can complete more deliveries per day and earn more per shift.
Needs simple proof-of-delivery capture — quick photo + status update, not a complex multi-step form.
Wants earnings and COD collection to be transparently tracked so disputes are easily resolved.
O
The Operations Manager
Dispatch / In-Charge
Needs to assign and monitor hundreds of orders per day without manual coordination overhead.
Wants real-time visibility into every rider's location and progress to proactively manage SLA risk.
Needs fraud controls — geo-fences, status locks, signature capture — to prevent false delivery claims.
Wants performance analytics by rider, zone, and time period to optimize staffing and coverage.
C
The Customer
End Recipient
Wants to know exactly when their parcel will arrive — without calling the merchant or courier helpline.
Needs a live tracking link so they can be home or redirect delivery if plans change.
Wants ETA notifications that are actually accurate — vague "arriving today" messages erode trust.
Expects a simple way to confirm receipt and provide feedback on the delivery experience.

Proprietary Innovation

Rupantor — the algorithm
we built from scratch.

The centrepiece of Optimus is Rupantor — a proprietary area-selection priority queue algorithm developed by the Barikoi engineering team. Named after the Bengali word for "transformer," Rupantor solves the fundamental inefficiency of delivery dispatch: assigning the right order to the right rider at the right time, based on geography rather than availability alone.

Standard delivery systems assign orders first-come-first-served or by manual dispatcher judgment. Rupantor instead segments all pending orders into geographic clusters, scores each cluster by urgency and density, and assigns them to riders whose current position and remaining capacity makes them the optimal match — minimizing total distance travelled across the entire fleet simultaneously, not just for individual orders.

01
Geographic Clustering
All pending orders are clustered into delivery zones using Barikoi's proprietary address segmentation. Orders within the same cluster are batched together — ensuring a rider never criss-crosses the city for adjacent deliveries.
02
Priority Scoring
Each cluster is scored by a weighted combination of order age (time since creation), delivery SLA urgency, cluster density (number of orders per km²), and merchant priority tier. The highest-scoring cluster gets dispatched first.
03
Optimal Rider Matching
Rupantor matches each cluster to the rider whose current GPS position, remaining carrying capacity, and shift time remaining makes them the lowest-cost executor — minimising total fleet distance, not just individual trip distance.
Cost Reduction Strategy

The Box Model that cut
costs by 15%.

Beyond intelligent routing, Optimus introduced a structural change to how deliveries are physically organized — the Box Model. Instead of riders carrying a mixed bag of orders sorted loosely by memory, each rider's load is pre-sorted into physical delivery zones before the shift begins, matching Rupantor's digital clustering with a physical analogue.

Before — Traditional Model
Mixed Load, Reactive Routing
  • Orders loaded in arrival order, no geographic sorting
  • Rider plans route mentally at start of shift
  • Backtracking common — same area visited 2–3× per shift
  • Failed delivery attempts not rescheduled intelligently
  • Fuel costs unpredictable, no per-route visibility
After — Box Model
Zone-Sorted Load, Optimized Route
  • Orders pre-sorted into zone boxes before shift starts
  • Rupantor generates optimized route matching the sorted load
  • Each zone completed in one pass — zero backtracking
  • Failed attempts auto-rescheduled to the next same-zone shift
  • Per-shift fuel cost tracked and compared against baseline
System Design

How Optimus
actually works.

Optimus runs across three layers simultaneously — the merchant-facing web dashboard for order management and analytics, the rider mobile app for trip execution and proof-of-delivery, and the operations dashboard for dispatch, monitoring, and financial reconciliation.

Phase 1 Merchant — Order Creation
1.1
Order Entry
Merchant creates a delivery order via the web dashboard or API integration — entering customer name, delivery address, parcel details, COD amount (if applicable), and any special instructions. Bulk upload via CSV supported for high-volume merchants.
Actor: Merchant · Platform: Web Dashboard or API · Trigger: Order creation → Geocoding
1.2
Address Geocoding & Fraud Pre-Check
The system immediately geocodes the delivery address using Barikoi Maps and cross-checks it against the fraud detection engine — flagging anomalous addresses, suspicious order frequency, or payment method irregularities before the order is accepted into the dispatch queue.
Actor: System · Technology: Barikoi Maps API + Fraud Engine · Output: Verified order in dispatch queue
↓
Phase 2 System — Rupantor Dispatch
2.1
Area Clustering & Priority Scoring
Rupantor groups all pending orders into geographic clusters and scores each cluster by urgency, density, and SLA status. High-priority clusters (time-sensitive, high-density, or premium merchant tier) are elevated in the dispatch queue.
Actor: System · Algorithm: Rupantor Priority Queue · Inputs: Order age, GPS coordinates, cluster density, merchant tier
2.2
Rider Matching & Load Assignment
Rupantor identifies the optimal rider for each cluster based on current GPS position, remaining carrying capacity, and shift time remaining. The matched rider receives a push notification with their assigned cluster — pre-sorted into the Box Model zone order.
Actor: System → Rider · Output: Zone-sorted trip list + pre-optimized route pushed to rider app
↓
Phase 3 Rider — Trip Execution
3.1
Accept Assignment & Review Route
Rider opens the mobile app, reviews the assigned cluster of orders plotted on the map with the optimized route overlaid, and accepts the shift. All orders are pre-sorted in delivery sequence — no manual planning needed.
Actor: Rider · Platform: iOS / Android App
3.2
Navigate with Live Traffic
Rider follows the Optimus-generated route with live traffic data integrated. If a road is blocked or conditions change, the route auto-recalculates and the customer's ETA is updated in real time — triggering a push notification to the customer when the rider is 500m away.
Actor: Rider + Customer · Technology: Barikoi Maps + Live Traffic API
3.3
Proof of Delivery Capture
At each delivery point, the rider's delivery status button only unlocks when GPS confirms they are within 100 metres of the address. Rider photographs the delivered parcel (in-app camera only — no gallery uploads), captures digital signature if required, and scans barcode for additional confirmation. All evidence is geo-stamped and timestamped automatically.
Actor: Rider · Fraud Control: Status lock until within 100m · Evidence: Photo + GPS + Timestamp + Optional Signature
3.4
COD Collection & Reconciliation
For cash-on-delivery orders, the rider records the collected amount in-app at the point of handover. The system cross-checks this against the order value and flags any discrepancy immediately to the operations dashboard. End-of-shift COD reconciliation is automated.
Actor: Rider · Finance Control: Real-time COD discrepancy alert to Operations Dashboard
↓
Phase 4 Operations — Monitoring & QA
4.1
Live Fleet Visibility
Operations dashboard shows every active rider's real-time GPS position, current order status, and remaining trip count. Geo-fence alerts trigger if a rider marks a delivery complete while outside the target address zone. In-charges can reassign orders between riders in real time if a rider goes offline or hits capacity.
Actor: Operations Manager / In-Charge · Feature: Live map + Geo-fence enforcement + Manual reassignment
4.2
Performance & Financial Analytics
The analytics dashboard tracks deliveries per rider per day, delivery success rate by zone, average cost per delivery versus target, COD collection efficiency, and merchant satisfaction scores. Finance teams access profit margin by zone and expense breakdowns — all updated in real time, not batch-processed overnight.
Actor: Operations Manager / Finance Team · Metrics: Deliveries/rider/day · Cost per delivery · Zone margin · COD reconciliation
↓
Phase 5 Merchant — Confirmation & Reporting
5.1
Delivery Confirmation & Customer Feedback
Once a delivery is marked complete, the merchant dashboard updates in real time — showing proof-of-delivery photos, GPS confirmation, and timestamp. The customer receives a delivery confirmation with a one-tap feedback prompt. Feedback aggregates into the merchant's delivery quality score.
Actor: Merchant + Customer · Output: Proof-of-delivery record + Customer feedback score
5.2
Revenue Reconciliation & Reporting
End-of-day reports are automatically generated for merchants — showing total deliveries, failed attempts, COD collected vs. outstanding, and charges due. Finance teams receive a parallel P&L summary by delivery zone with comparison against prior periods.
Actor: Merchant + Finance Team · Output: Auto-generated daily delivery + financial report

PM Reflection

What I learned
building Optimus.

Serving multiple personas in one product is the hardest design problem. A feature that delights the merchant (mandatory delivery photos) can frustrate the rider (extra steps at the door). Every trade-off had to be adjudicated explicitly — not left to engineering to decide. I ran separate feedback sessions with each persona before every major release.

The proprietary algorithm was our real moat, not the features. Any logistics SaaS can offer order management and tracking. Rupantor — built on Barikoi's unique address data and geographic segmentation — was something no competitor could copy quickly. I pushed hard to prioritize it as the core differentiator, not just one feature among many.

COD reconciliation is the number one merchant pain point — everywhere. I initially underestimated how central cash-on-delivery was to the Bangladeshi F-commerce economy. Once we built real-time COD tracking and end-of-day auto-reconciliation, merchant retention jumped immediately. Always follow the money flow in your users' businesses.

Physical and digital systems must be designed together. The Box Model isn't a software feature — it's a change to how riders physically load their bikes before a shift. Getting operations managers to adopt it required training, change management, and a two-week parallel-running period. Product work doesn't stop at the app.
